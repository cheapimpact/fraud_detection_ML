mendorong bank sentral memperketat kebijakan moneter.

meningkat, yang pada akhirnya menghambat investasi di

sebelumnya mencapai puncaknya di 9,4% pada kuartal ketiga 
2022.1

pada pasar utang negara, perlambatan ekonomi di Tiongkok,

Global and National Economy
The global economy in 2024 continued to face significant 
challenges. The post-Covid-19 pandemic recovery has yet to 
fully materialize, leaving long-term scarring effects in many 
countries and hampering economic growth. Inflation in several 
countries remains relatively high, prompting central banks to 
tighten monetary policy. Aggressive interest rate hikes have led 
to increased borrowing costs, ultimately constraining investment 
across various industrial sectors. In its 2024 World Economic 
Outlook report, the International Monetary Fund (IMF) projected 
that the global fight against inflation is nearing its end. Global 
inflation is forecasted to decline to 3.5% by the end of 2025, after 
previously peaking at 9.4% in the third quarter of 2022.1
However, the IMF also stated that global economic risks remain 
high due to several factors, such as prolonged geopolitical 
conflicts, financial market volatility affecting the sovereign 
debt market, the economic slowdown in China, and the rise 
of protectionist policies in various countries. Additionally, the 
wave of elections in several nations also contributes to global 
economic uncertainty.
Despite facing various risks, the global economy has continued 
to demonstrate resilience, successfully avoiding a global 
recession. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) projects that 
global economic growth will remain stable at 3.2% in 2024–2025, 
slightly better than 3.1% in 2023, but still below the average 
growth rate prior to the Covid-19 pandemic. The uneven nature 
of the post-pandemic economic recovery has left long-term 
scarring effects in many countries, particularly in low-income 
nations.
According to the World Bank report titled Fiscal Vulnerabilities 
in Low-Income Countries, 26 of the most economically 
disadvantaged nations, home to 40% of the world’s low-income 
population, are currently facing the highest loan rates since 
2006. This situation has left them vulnerable to natural disasters 
and other economic shocks. The overall economic performance 
of these countries is weaker than it was prior to the COVID-19 
pandemic, amplifying inequalities and slowing the pace of global 
recovery.2

According to data from the Ministry of Communications and 
Digital, the length of the Submarine Cable Communication System 
(SKKL) fiber optic network has reached 118,257.54 kilometers, while 
land-based fiber optic infrastructure spans 716,935.55 kilometers. 
This network now covers 34 provinces, 503 cities/regencies, 
5,564 districts, and 47,483 villages, with land fiber optics having 
grown by more than 269% since December 2019. The fiber optic 
network is a key infrastructure for delivering high-speed internet, 
serving as the backbone, middle mile, and last mile, while also 
supporting Base Transceiver Station (BTS) backhaul for the cellular 
telecommunications network.6
4G technology now covers nearly all residential areas in 
Indonesia. However, the disparity in coverage between urban 
and rural areas remains a challenge, particularly in remote 
regions that require special solutions, such as the use of satellite 
technology. The 5G network, although still in its early stages, 
covering only 2.90% of residential areas, has immense potential 
to enhance the speed and quality of internet services. In line 
with technological advancements and infrastructure expansion, 
5G coverage is expected to grow, supporting the diverse digital 
needs of the Indonesian people in the years to come.
The equal distribution of digital infrastructure is a fundamental 
foundation for enhancing Indonesia’s digital competitiveness. 
Based on the Digital Competitiveness Index by East Ventures, 
regions on Java Island still dominate the highest scores for 
digital competitiveness, due to a more even distribution of digital 
infrastructure compared to other areas. Outside of Java, Southeast 
Sulawesi ranks among the top eight regions with the highest digital 
competitiveness score, driven by digital infrastructure projects 
and the presence of the Palapa Ring Middle Package, which has 
accelerated internet access and connectivity in the region.7

Capital Structure and Management Policy 
on Capital Structure
The capital structure is a combination of own capital (equity) and 
debt (liabilities). A capital structure with a minimum cost of using 
funds can affect an increase in the value of a company’s shares, 
but does not increase earnings per share.
The Company seeks to intensify its capital structure by 
optimizing the minimum weighted average capital. The increase 
in the value of the Company’s shares can be influenced by the 
capital structure with a minimum cost of using funds. However, 
this still cannot increase earnings per share.
The main objective of the Company’s capital management is 
to ensure that the Company maintains healthy capital ratios 
in order to support the business and maximize shareholder 
value. The Company manages the capital structure and makes 
adjustments to the capital structure in light of changes in 
economic conditions. The capital structure policy is also aimed at 
shareholders by determining the price of products and services 
that are commensurate with the level of risk.
As is generally accepted practice, the Company evaluates its 
capital structure through an analysis of the debt-to-equity ratio,
