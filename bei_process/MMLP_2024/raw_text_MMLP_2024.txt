In 2024, the global economy continued to face 
uncertainty driven by various external factors, including 
prolonged geopolitical conflicts, disruptions in global 
supply chains, and an ongoing energy crisis. The 
Russia-Ukraine conflict, tensions in the Middle East, 
and instability in several other regions have exerted 
additional pressure on global economic growth. Amid 
these conditions, global inflation remained elevated, 
although it showed a slight decline compared to the 
previous year.
The International Monetary Fund (IMF), in its World 
Economic Outlook report published in January 2025, 
projected global economic growth at 3.2% for 2024, 
slightly lower than the 3.1% recorded in the previous 
year. The United States experienced a slowdown in 
economic growth, from 3.2% in 2023 to 2.4% in 2024, 
despite the Federal Reserve cutting interest rates by 
a total of 100 basis points over the course of the year. 
Meanwhile, Europe faced stagnation, with Germany 
contracting by 0.2% and France by 0.1%. On the other 
hand, China maintained a growth rate of 5.4%, although 
this remained below the pre-pandemic average of 
6–7%.
Amid

challenges, 
Indonesia’s 
economy 
continued 
to 
demonstrate 
resilience. 
National

positive trend above 5% since 2022. This stability was 
underpinned by the synergistic policy coordination 
between the Government and Bank Indonesia, which 
successfully maintained household purchasing power 
and controlled inflation at 1.57%. Furthermore, domestic 
economic resilience was reinforced by a stable rupiah 
exchange rate and increased investment in the 
technology and infrastructure sectors.
