GLOBAL ECONOMY GROWING 
MODERATELY, INFLATION DECLINING 
DESPITE INCREASING RISKS 
The World Bank in its January 2024 Global Economic 
Prospects (GEP) projected that global growth would 
slow down from 2.9% in 2023 to 2.4% in 2024. This 
forecast reflected concerns over the sustained impact 
of tight monetary policies aimed at controlling 
inflation, constrained credit conditions, as well as 
weak global trade and investment.
Meanwhile, the International Monetary Fund (IMF) in 
its January 2024 World Economic Outlook (WEO) 
estimated that global growth in 2024 would remain 
at around 3.1%—the same level as in 2023 according 
to the IMF. Compared to October 2023 WEO, this 
projection rose by approximately 0.2 percentage 
points, due to improved growth prospects in China, 
the United States, as well as emerging market and 
developing economies.
A key focus for the global economy in 2024 was 
the state of inflation and the response of various 
central banks in managing benchmark interest rates, 
particularly the US Federal Reserve. The surge in 
inflation, notably in the US and Europe, began with 
Russia-Ukraine war in February 2022, which triggered 
a spike in energy prices. In response to the inflationary 
surge, several central banks raised benchmark interest 
rates starting in mid-2022. These elevated rates were 
maintained throughout 2023 as inflation remained 
high. In 2024, US inflation declined from 6% at the 
beginning of 2023 to a range of 2.5–3% by year-end. 
Similarly, Eurozone countries experienced inflation fall 
from 8% to around 2.5% within the same period.
At the end of 2023, The Fed announced its plan to 
lower the benchmark interest rate. However, this plan 
was only implemented in mid-September 2024, when 
the US benchmark rate was reduced from 5.5% to 5%. 
The moderation of US inflation provided the basis for 
the Fed’s decision to cut rates. The Fed further reduced

influenced by weakening domestic consumption and 
purchasing power, which led to a slowdown in the

RENEWABLE ENERGY DEVELOPMENT
Indonesia has set a target to achieve Net Zero 
Emissions (NZE) by 2060 or earlier, with the energy 
transition framework centred on six fundamental 
pillars: energy efficiency, electrification of motor 
vehicles, utilization of renewable energy, development 
of new energy sources (nuclear, hydrogen and green 
ammonia), a moratorium on new coal-fired power 
plants (PLTU) and phase-out of existing PLTU, as well 
as carbon capture storage (CCS/CCUS). These pillars 
reflect Indonesia’s commitment to climate change 
mitigation and sustainable energy practices.

On February 16, 2024, PAMA inaugurated the Off-Grid PLTS project built by EPN 
at the PAMA MTBU operational site in South Sumatra. This project features an 
innovative installation of a PLTS with a capacity of 615.6 kWp, utilizing a 200 kWh 
power storage battery. This marks the first implementation of the Smart Micro Grid 
concept in Indonesia, combined with a battery. By applying a Photovoltaic (PV) 
ratio to the Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) of 2:1 within the microgrid, the 
system can increase the proportion of renewable energy by 30% with the same BESS 
configuration.
This system can handle off-grid disturbances, prevent losses from power outages 
amounting to US$ 0.5 million per 10MW, and includes a Smart String-level 
disconnection system, enhancing the security of the PV system. The installation of the 
Off-Grid PLTS at the PAMA MTBU Site can reduce carbon emissions in coal mining 
areas by 423,000 tons of CO2 per year, equivalent to planting 19,000 trees annually.

were still influenced by fluctuations in global 
commodity prices and the slowdown in execution 
of construction projects ahead of the 2024 General 
Election. Most businesses using heavy equipment 
adopted a wait-and-see approach while awaiting 
the General Election results, the new government’s 
infrastructure policies, and global commodity price 
developments.
Fluctuations 
in

have 
made

in purchasing heavy equipment. This has made 
market competition even tighter. The influx of Chinese 
products has made competition in the market 
increasingly fierce and led to harmful price wars.
The Company chose to implement a holistic approach 
that emphasizes added value as a comprehensive 
solution for customers and differentiates it from 
competitors.
By the end of 2024, Komatsu’s heavy equipment

compared to 5,270 units in 2023. UD Trucks sales fell

Scania’s sales fell 39% from 715 units to 436 units due 
to declining demand, especially in the mining sector.

PERFORMANCE IN 2024
The decline in global coal prices since early 2024 has 
had an impact on the decline in the income of coal 
companies, including Turangga Resources. 
Environmental issues in developed countries that 
drive the transition to renewable energy have reduced 
dependence on coal and depressed global demand. 
However, until now there has been no renewable 
energy source that can significantly replace coal 
supply for electricity generation so that the prospect 
of coal still exists.
In 2024, thermal and metallurgical coal sales volume 
amounted to 13.1 million tons, increased by 11% 
compared to 11.8 million tons in 2023. However, the 
decline in coal prices reduced Turangga Resources’ 
revenue by 15% from Rp30.5 trillion in 2023 to Rp26.0 
trillion.

replacement of benchmark interest rates such as the 
United States Dollar (US$) London Interbank Offered 
Rate (“LIBOR”) and other Interbank Offered Rates 
(“IBOR”) have become a priority for global regulators.
Currently, the Company has a number of outstanding 
borrowings which refer to the Jakarta Interbank 
Offered Rate (“JIBOR”). The Company is still discussing 
with the creditors on the borrowing contract which 
refer to JIBOR. The Company is in the opinion that 
no significant impact on the Company’s consolidated 
financial statements as the JIBOR is still available until 
December 31, 2025.
